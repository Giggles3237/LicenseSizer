# How Dealerships Can Collect Driver’s Licenses Online Without Chasing Blurry Text Messages

Collecting a customer’s driver’s license should be simple.

In practice, it often turns into a chain of text messages, downloads, screenshots, cropping, resizing, rotating, and asking the customer to try again.

The customer sends a photo from across the room. The license is sitting on a kitchen counter. Part of the card is covered by glare. The image is sideways. The front is usable, but the back is missing.

Then someone at the dealership has to clean it up before it can be used.

That process may only take a few minutes each time, but across an entire sales team, those minutes add up.

A better process gives the customer a clear, guided way to create a clean license copy from their phone without requiring the dealership employee to manually fix the image afterward.

## Why text-message collection creates extra work

Texting is convenient, but it was not designed for consistent document collection.

When a salesperson asks a customer to text a photo of a driver’s license, the dealership has very little control over what comes back.

Common problems include:

- The photo is blurry.
- The license is too small in the frame.
- The image is rotated incorrectly.
- There is glare across the card.
- The background takes up most of the photo.
- Only the front of the license is provided.
- The image is sent in a format that is awkward to print or save.
- The employee has to crop and resize the image manually.
- The customer has to be contacted again.

The result is an inconsistent process that depends on the customer taking a good photo and the employee knowing how to repair it.

## The hidden cost of a small task

Manually fixing one license photo may not feel like a serious operational problem.

The problem is repetition.

Suppose a dealership processes 100 customer license copies in a month. If each one takes only three minutes to download, rotate, crop, resize, convert, and save, that is five hours of repetitive administrative work.

That estimate does not include:

- Repeated requests for better photos
- Missing front or back images
- Time spent searching through text-message threads
- Confusion about where the final file was saved
- Delays before a remote appointment or delivery

The real cost is not just the employee time. It is the interruption.

Every time a salesperson stops what they are doing to clean up a photo, they lose focus on the customer, the deal, or the next task.

## What a better license-collection workflow looks like

A cleaner process should be simple for both sides.

The dealership should be able to:

1. Send the customer one branded link.
2. Give the customer clear instructions.
3. Receive a consistent document format.
4. Avoid manually cropping or resizing the photo.
5. Reduce the need for follow-up messages.

The customer should be able to:

1. Open the link on a phone.
2. Photograph the license.
3. Review the result.
4. Create a clean PDF.
5. Share the finished document with the dealership.

The goal is not to make the customer learn a new system. The goal is to remove the unnecessary steps that usually happen after the customer takes the photo.

## Why consistency matters

Dealership processes become difficult when every employee handles the same task differently.

One salesperson may save license photos to a desktop folder. Another may email them to themselves. Another may leave them in a text thread. Someone else may take a screenshot and print it.

A standardized workflow creates a more predictable result.

That makes it easier to:

- Train new employees
- Handle remote customers
- Prepare for appointments
- Support BDC and internet sales teams
- Reduce last-minute document requests
- Keep the customer experience consistent

The value is not simply a better-looking license image. The value is a repeatable process.

## Privacy should be considered from the beginning

A driver’s license contains sensitive personal information.

Dealerships should avoid unnecessary copying, storage, or sharing of license images. They should also understand what happens to the customer’s image during the collection process.

Useful questions to ask any document-collection provider include:

- Is the original image stored?
- Where is it processed?
- Is it uploaded to a third-party server?
- How long is it retained?
- Who can access it?
- What information is included in analytics or tracking tools?
- Does the system verify identity, or does it only prepare the document?

A document-preparation tool should be clear about what it does and what it does not do.

## Where this workflow is most useful

A guided license-collection process can be especially helpful for:

### Remote vehicle purchases

When the customer is not physically present, document collection can quickly become a series of messages and attachments. A guided link gives the customer a clear next step.

### Appointment preparation

A dealership can collect the license before the customer arrives, helping the team prepare in advance.

### BDC and internet leads

BDC representatives can send the same approved link instead of giving different instructions each time.

### Home delivery

A clean document-collection process can reduce last-minute back-and-forth before delivery.

### Busy showroom traffic

Even when the customer is in the store, a simple mobile workflow can reduce the need to scan, copy, or manually resize a license at a desk.

## What to look for in a dealership license-collection tool

Before choosing a solution, evaluate the full workflow rather than only the image-capture feature.

Look for:

- A simple mobile experience
- Clear customer instructions
- Support for the front and back of the license
- Consistent PDF output
- Dealership branding
- A clear privacy explanation
- Minimal setup
- No unnecessary customer account creation
- No manual image editing by dealership employees

The best tool is the one employees will actually use and customers can complete without assistance.

## Start with one repeatable process

Dealerships do not need to overhaul every document workflow at once.

Start with one common task: collecting a usable driver’s-license copy.

Create a standard process for when the link is sent, who sends it, and where the completed PDF should go.

For example:

1. The customer schedules an appointment.
2. The salesperson sends the dealership’s LicenseResizer link.
3. The customer completes the capture before arriving.
4. The finished PDF is shared with the appropriate dealership employee.
5. The employee follows the dealership’s normal document-handling procedure.

A small, repeatable process is easier to adopt than a large technology rollout.
